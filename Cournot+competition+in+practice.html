Research question and subquestions ¶

In this assignment, we will look at several forms of Cournot competition. Cournot competition is also known as quantity competition. We will look at standard Cournot competition and Stackelberg application of Cournot competition. We will start with a short introduction about the theory behind the models, after which we will focus on the applications. We will look at both a symmetric and asymmetric two firm case for both models. Our research question will be:

  • Which model of Cournot competition is preferred?

This question will be separated into two subquestions, for which we will show which party favors which form of competition. The subquestions of the research thus are:

  • Which model of Cournot competition is favored by each firm?

  • Which model of Cournot competition gives the highest consumer surplus?

In this assignment, we consider a duopoly in which both firms decide the quantity $q$ of its products. The reaction function of inverse (consumer) demand then determines the market price. We compare the price resulting from the quantities chosen by the firms in each scenario to find the price which gives the highest consumer surplus. In our conclusion we answer these questions.

Motivation and introduction¶

Cournot competition is a repetitive theory in the field of economics. This assignment aims at teaching students, who are new to Cournot competition, the standard theory and prediction and the use of multiple models. Our research question is relevant for economics to provide economic intuition behind game theory and the importance of the use of economic models. The subquestions also aim to show that there is no single market set-up which is preferred by all players of the game.

To introduce the theory behind the Cournot model, we take the book Game Theory for Applied Economists (A Primer in Game Theory) by Robert Gibbons (2002, Princeton University Press) as a base and rewrite the terms to our model. All references to theory will thus be made against this piece of literature. We furthermore assume that readers of this piece are already familiar with basic economic reasoning, such as price will be determined by the point where supply equals demand. For readers who lack this knowledge, we strongly advise to first read the introductory chapter by Gibbons.

Simple model of Cournot competition¶

We consider a very simple version of Cournot's model. Let $q_i$ and $q_j$ denote the quantities (of a homogeneous product) produced by firm $i$ and firm $j$, respectively. Let $P(Q)$ be the market clearing price when the aggregate quantity on the market is $Q = q_i + q_j$. More precisely, $P(Q) = a - Q$ for $Q < a$, and $P(Q) = 0$ for $ Q \ge a$. Assume that the total cost of firm $i$ ($j$) of producing quantity $i$ ($j$) is $C_i(q_i) = c_i*q_i$ ($C_j(q_j) = c_i*q_j$). That is, there are no fixed costs for either firm and marginal cost is constant at $c$, where $c$ is assumed to be smaller than $a$. Following the standard Cournot model, both firms will simultaneously choose quantities.

Stackelberg model of Cournot competition¶

Contrary to the standard model of Cournot competition explained above, in a Stackelberg model the firms will not choose quantities simultaneously. Instead, we will have a Stackelberg leader and a Stackelberg follower. The timing is as follows. In period 1, the Stackelberg leader will choose its quantity. In period 2, the Stackelberg follower will choose its quantity based on the quantity produced by the leader. In this model, the leader will have a stronger position as it knows that the follower will set quantity such to maximize its own profits. As a result, it pays to be the leader in the Stackelberg model. This is something we aim to show below.

Method and assumptions¶

By modeling and defining relevant variables, we will find answers to our main research question and our subquestions. By changing the parameter values, we will also do a sensitivy analysis.

To answer the research question and subquestions, we make a couple of assumptions. First of all, we assume that there are only two firms in the market, maximizing their own profit. It is common knowledge that all players are rational and both firms produce a homogeneous good, so we have a case of perfect substitutes. The profit function of each player is strictly concave and in the symmetric case, discussed below, we assume homogeneous costs. In the asymmetric case however, we assume heterogeneity in costs. For all cases we assume the constant $a$ in the inverse market demand equation to be equal for all firms. Lastly, we assume that there are no fixed costs.

Standard two firm case¶

In the standard two firm case there are two firms, firm $i$ and firm $j$. We will start with defining the specifics of the two firm game. Here total quantity produced is the sum of the quantity of firm $i$ and firm $j$, so: $Q = q_i + q_j$. The profit of the firm equals total revenue (price times own quantity produced) minus total costs (marginal costs times own quantity produced). The best-response function is calculated by setting the FOC equal to zero.

Inverse demand function: $ p(Q) = max(a - Q; 0) = a - (q_i + q_j) $

Cost functions: $ C_i(q_i) = c_i*q_i $

Number of players: $ I = 2 $

Strategy sets: $S_i = [0; \infty); i = 1; 2 $

Payoff functions: $\pi_i(q_i; q_j) = (p - c_i) * q_i = (a - (q_i + q_j) - c_i)q_i; i = 1; 2; i \neq j $

First order condition ($FOC$): $\pi_i' = a - 2q_i - q_j - c_i $

Best-response function firm i: $q_i = 1/2(a - q_j - c_i) $

Best-response function firm j: $q_j = 1/2(a - q_i - c_j) $

Cournot competition: Symmetric and Asymmetric¶

In Cournot competition, both firms will choose quantity simultaneously. In that way, no firm can set their quantity as a response to the other firm's chosen quantity. Instead, the quantity they will choose will be based on the best-response function of each firm. We will only work out the general best-response for each firm in Cournot competition as the application of the different cost structures can be easily filled in. We use all standard equations described above. The applications will be as follows:

  • Symmetric Cournot competition: both firms have the same marginal costs ($c_i = c_j = 10$). Therefore, both profit and best-response functions will be the same. We will refer to this scenario as Cournot symmetric.
  • Asymmetric Cournot competition: firm i is the low-cost firm and firm j is the high-cost firm ($c_i = 10$ and $c_{jh} = 25$). The profit function of firm $i$ will not change in comparison with the symmetric case, but the profit function of firm $j$ will change due to the changed marginal costs. Below we will show the profit functions in a graph and compare the two scenarios. We will refer to this scenario as Cournot asymmetric.

Solving the standard Cournot Game¶

In the first case, both firms have identical marginal costs of $10$. Both firms will simultaneously choose quantities and thus anticipate the behavior of the other firms. As there is complete information, both firms know the profit function of the other firm and with this knowledge firms choose their own quantity.

Firm $i$, anticipating optimal behavior by firm $j$, will maximize its $\pi_i$ by setting $q_i$ accordingly.

$\pi_i(q_i, q_j) = (a - q_i - q_j(q_i) - c_i)q_i = (a - q_i - q_j - c_i)q_i $

To find the optimal quantity of firm $i$, we look for the point where marginal profit is equal to zero, after which we will describe $q_i$ as a function of the marginal costs of each firm.

$FOC_i: \pi_i'(q_i, q_j) = a - 2q_i - q_j - c_i = o $

$q_i = 1/2(a - q_j - c_i)$

One can retrieve the best-response of $q_j$ to $q_i$ in the exact same way. Due to symmetry of the profit functions of both firms we will skip the simple mathematics shown above and simply state $q_j = 1/2(a - q_i - c_j)$.

Substituting $q_j$ into $q_i$ will give us the equilibrium $q_i*$:

$q_i = 1/2(a - 1/2(a - q_i - c_j) - c_i)$

$q_i = 1/2(a - 1/2a + 1/2q_i + 1/2c_j - c_i)$

$q_i = 1/2(1/2a + 1/2q_i + 1/2c_j - c_i)$

$2q_i = 1/2a + 1/2q_i - c_i + 1/2c_j$

$1.5q_i = 1/2a - c_i + 1/2c_j$

$1.5q_i = 1/2(a - 2c_i + c_j)$

$q_i* = 1/3(a - 2c_i + c_j)$

Due to symmetry, $q_j* = 1/3(a + c_i - 2c_j)$.

The intersections of the red line with the blue line (symmetric) and with the cyan line (asymmetric) can also be mathematically calculated by setting the best-response functions equal to eachother. Stackelberg: Symmetric and Asymmetric¶

In the Stackelberg model there is quantity competition, as in the Cournot model described above. However, now the firms do not make decisions simultaneously. In the Stackelberg game there is a dominant firm, called the Stackelberg leader, and a weak firm, the Stackelberg follower. We will describe three cases in this section.

  • Symmetric Stackelberg: the dominant firm and the weak firm have the same marginal costs. We will refer to this scenario as case 1.
  • Asymmetric Stackelberg 1: the dominant firm is the low-cost firm and the Stackelberg follower is the high-cost firm. We will refer to this scenario as case 2.
  • Asymmetric Stackelberg 2: the Stackelberg leader is the high-cost firm, whereas the weak firm is the low-cost firm. We will refer to this scenario as case 3.

Solving the Stackelberg Game¶

In case 1, both firms have identical marginal costs of $10$. Firm $i$ is the dominant firm and firm $j$ is the weaker following firm. This model thus has two periods: leading firm $i$ sets its quantity in period 1 and the following firm $j$ sets its quantity in period 2, observing the quantity of the dominant firm $q_i$. This game can be solved by backward induction, which means we'll solve the second stage first. The marginal costs, demand function and profit function are the same as mentioned in the standard case. Firm $j$ will adjust its $q_j$ to $q_i$ by using its best-response function. From now on we'll use subscript $l$ to indicate the leading firm and subscript $f$ to indicate the following firm.

The best-response function of the following firm remains the same as in the standard case, i.e.: $ q_f = 1/2(a - q_l - c_f)$

Firm $l$, anticipating optimal behavior by firm $f$, will maximize its $\pi_l$ by setting $q_l$ accordingly.

$\pi_l(q_l; q_f) = (a - q_l - q_f(q_l) - c_l)q_l = (a - q_l - c_l - 1/2(a - q_l - c_f))q_l $

$= (a - q_l - c_l - 1/2a + 1/2q_l + 1/2c_f)q_l = (1/2a - 1/2q_l - c_l + 1/2c_f)q_l $

$FOC_l: \pi_l'(q_l; q_f) = 1/2a - q_l - c_l - + 1/2c_f = 0 $

$q_l* = 1/2(a - 2c_l + c_f)$

One can retrieve the equilibrium $q_f$ by filling in the $q_l*$ in the best-response function $q_f$.

$q_f = 1/2(a - 1/2(a - 2c_l + c_f) - c_f) = 1/2(a - 1/2a + c_l - 1/2c_f - c_f = 1/4a + 1/2c_l - 3/4c_f $

$q_f*= 1/4(a + 2c_l - 3c_f)$

In case 1, where $c_l = c_f = 10$ the $q_l*$ equals $1/2(a-c)$ and $q_f*$ equals $1/4(a-c)$. Below we will find the equilibrium quantities, price and profits using Python for all three cases.

In this section we compared a symmetric Stackelberg case with two asymmetric cases (one in which the leader is the low-cost firm (case 2) and one in which the leader is the high-cost firm (case 3) We see that:

(1) $q_{i2} > q_{i1} > q_{i3}$ and $q_{j3} > q_{j1} > q_{j2}$.

(2) $p_3* > p_2* > p_1*$

(3) $\pi_{i2} > \pi_{i1} > \pi_{i3}$ and $\pi_{j1} > \pi_{j3} > \pi_{j2}$

(4) $\pi_{T1} > \pi_{T2} > \pi_{T3}$

In all cases, we see that it pays to be a leader. In the first case, the leader $i$ has the highest quantity and profit. In case 2, the low-cost firm $i$ has an even higher quantity and profit than in case 1. Lastly, in case 3 we see that the high-cost firm $j$ can better be a leader than a follower, as in case 2. Total profits are highest in the homogeneous cost case. More information can be found in the conclusion.

Consumer surplus¶

We have seen what the different scenarios do to the equilibrium quantity, price and (total) profits. However, we do not know yet which scenario is best for consumers. We can find this out by calculating the so-called consumer surplus in all five scenarios. The formula for the consumer surplus is $CS = 1/2 * (a - p) * Q$.

To conclude this assignment, we will answer the research question and subquestions.

There is not one Cournot competition model that is preferred in all cases and for all groups.

  • Firm $i$ would prefer Stackelberg with asymmetric costs, in which it is the low-cost leading firm. That way, firm $i$ has the highest profits.
  • Firm $j$ would prefer the symmetric Cournot model, i.e. where firm $i$ and $j$ choose a quantity simultaneously.
  • In case firm $j$ is a high-cost firm, it prefers to be the leader.
  • Consumers would prefer the symmetric Stackelberg model, i.e. where firm $i$ (the leader) and firm $j$ (the follower) have the same marginal costs.

Discussion¶

In this assignment, we only looked at cases with two firms and with a change to one other cost ($c_j$ from $10$ to $c_{jh}$ of $25$). Of course a lot of other applications can be executed using Cournot and/or Stackelberg games. For example, one can look at applications with more than two firms or even with $n$ firms. One can also look at scenarios where there are multiple firms with multiple different marginal costs. Of course, this would change the results in relevant variables. One could also look into a case where there are fixed costs. For some variables this may not change, since fixed costs would disappear if one takes the derivative of the profit.

Since this sort of quantity competition is very important in economics, it is always interesting to look at more developed scenarios and other applications.
